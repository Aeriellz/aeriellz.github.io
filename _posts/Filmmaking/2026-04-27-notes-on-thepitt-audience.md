Once you write something, once you make a show and put it out there, it's not yours anymore. You lose most if not all of your ability to steer the direction things take from there. Press, marketing and a shit ton of money can only do so much to influence how people perceive your creation or how they'll engage with it, believe me. Some things are just not up to you.

It's always been like that, and creative types learn very quickly that it's best if you let that river run it's course. All you hope for is that it will move people. Big emotions in reaction to a story are not just fine, they're usually what the storyteller is dreaming about. They'll write a few words in a screen, spent 9 months translating it to reality, and the rest of their lives hoping it *did something.*

*The Pitt* does something, alright.

Many interesting choices were made there that support the show's themes and make it art, some of them plain to see, others more subtle and telling of a level of artistic maturity you only reach once you've been doing this for decades. It's a fascinating piece of media; intricate, but also really simple. Unlike, say, Severance, it's not *cerebral* writing, even if the medicine is accurate and fairly realistic. Instead, the show focuses on the *emotion* of it, puts you in the pressure pot and simmers you along with the characters, in a display of brilliance and courage that is rare nowadays.

It's no wonder the fandom gets... carried away.

### Something rotten

A while back I received a message with a link to a Tumblr post. It was a very strongly worded couple of paragraphs written by someone who was very clearly distressed and appalled that some people out there were wishing Robby would just jump off the hospital roof already.

My first reactions was... wait, there's people doing *what*?
 
All in all, it matters very little. These people are all *pretend* people. Nothing is real, and no one is going to come for you for murder if you think Robby needs to just take the quick way down and end his and everyone else's misery. The internet is, also, written in the language of hyperbole, specially in fan spaces like Tumblr. Wishing a character dead is a bit harsh, sure, specially a character clearly dealing with severe mental health issues. But it's hardly a matter of life and death. Besides, pretend or not, these storylines are so powerful that they instill very real feelings in people. Who's to say which buttons this pressed in your head?

Even without outright being told by the team in an interview or not, it's very clear to anyone with eyes and a semester of literature classes: this show is about people working under immense amounts of pressure and stress. The story is designed to put your there, in that same misery.

Of course you feel like shit about all of it. That's *the point*.

The whole thing would've left my mind without a second thought if it wasn't for the fact that wishing a character dead is incredibly tame and sane in comparison to some of the other things I've seen floating around.

Long story short, the discourse surrounding *The Pitt* in fan spaces is a cesspool of incomprehensible psychobabble and virtue signaling vocabulary. In-fighting in ways you would *not* believe. A melting pot of finger pointing, accusations, hate towards characters, other fans, and generally misguided misanthropy. Not a bunch that came together by the power of love and creativity, it feels more like a merry band of very opinionated people tied by the toxic joy of fighting with strangers on the internet.

In an age were there's so much talk about media literacy and the grammar of audiovisual language, I think we forgot that a good chunk of learning how to engage with media is emotional maturity and intelligence. It's not just about *thinking* through it, it's also leaning how to *feel* through it with the right part of our emotions. Back in college that was a good part of what the post-movie discussions taught me: you can't just use logic to rationalize your feelings. You have to parse them.

With *The Pitt*, taking the case of Tumblr as a space of debate, I've notice how most users have a tendency to not filter their first reactions through any sort of "well, but", unless it's about a situation or character who they already feel empathy towards, before expressing those reactions on the internet.

That filtering is inherently not easy, but social media made it *messy*, beyond being difficult. There's some weird shit happening to the relationship and hierarchy between expression and discussion nowadays: there's no clear distinction between them, when these are fundamentally different things. If I am venting about something I am not necessarily inviting someone to start a complex political discussion over it. And a single response on that regard is all it takes to ruin a bitch's day. When a space to be free becomes a space of struggle, nothing is safe.

Added to that, *The Pitt* is amongst a very special set of shows that could be taught as an example on how realistic structures and efforts can enhance a theme. Sometimes all you need is real life, because it is conflict enough. But because those storylines are *very* rooted in experiences that can be *very* real, it hits people in an equally real way.

It's a rich experience, and when the time then comes to express and discuss that experience, all we have is an environment where both of these things are unjustly conflated. People feel attacked, and any productive conclusion is stillborn in the wake of defensiveness and emotional turmoil.

An unsafe environment, and too many feeling... it's almost as if I've seen this before.

### The limits of empathy

What I take from the The Pitt fandom experiment is that empathy is a response with a lot of limits for most people, specially when it comes to challenging characters, those with unpleasant worldviews or those who degrade over time instead of having a classical or quick growth arc.

Camps of love and hate form quickly, and these reactions are usually monolithic. Most of the characters become vilified in some way by the fans at some point or another. If people's feelings are complex regarding them, the *posts* aren't. And you can only know what has been communicated.

I've rarely seen understanding and kindness as a response to characters who struggle, and, by struggling, cause someone harm, for example. And on the other hand, the fans who *favor* those challenging characters also have a hard time comprehending their limits and why they would pose challenges to other people—or they very quickly dismiss that understanding in order to just focus on what they love.

Robby and Trinity, particularly (and now Baran), are often taken in by the fans as a monolith of good or bad when their characters are precisely used as a discussion of grey areas, which can be very frustrating. Responses to them are usually hyperbolic and unfiltered in nature, and discourse starts, more often than not, on top of posts those responses, which were failed premise from the get go. From there we have a cycle of people reacting to one another, and dressing those naked reactions with a thin veil of social justice or expertise (which the only way to be heard or taken seriously in the interwebs.)

That's already not ideal. Too bad it gets worse. A lot of that lack of empathy towards character *does* translate into a lack of empathy towards the very real people in fandom, and I'm guilty of that myself. Instead of asking ourselves *hey, why would this character get this response out of you*, we react without filter again, presuming bad faith, or lack of understanding towards our side of the discussion.

It's all too fast, and it all feels too much like it has to happen *now*. Urgency is the first enemy of reasonableness.

A lot of the users are also specially young, and specially susceptible to lashing out. One because they're not emotionally intelligent and/or mature enough, yet, and two because of how social media has warped social discourse. Those who grew up in this environment are primed to be more reactive, and those who watched it become a monster don't remember they're equally bad at it. 

We all lack the antibodies to sustain life in the miserable landscape that became the internet, is what I'm saying. Fandom has been suffering a lot for it, and a space that used to be safe, I see a lot of people grieving for the changes that came with broader audiences and more attention.

Not to say that... everyone is tired. Real life has been a lot, and places of escape are a luxury. A lack of patience is, honestly, quite fair. Scratch that, it's just *fair as shit*. Because empathy *does* have a limit. At some point it drives you to the point of exhaustion and you stop being able to regulate your thoughts and emotions. If everyone reaches that breaing point, who's there to save us all? Is anyone even left?

That's the thematic crux behind *The Pitt*, if you think about it. Healthcare workers are particularly genius as a way to exemplify this. They're nothing if not exhausted by the need to be kind and selfless, the perfect image of a caretaker. Sometimes to people who they don't even think deserve care at all.

All of it takes a toll that is a lot more violent than we like to admit. The price of caring for someone else without time and space to care for yourself, or being taken care of, is higher than we like to admit.

The snowball from there is, to me, even more terrifying. If you know anyone close to you with depression or anxiety, or a more complex mental illnesses, you know that while they take the brunt of the harm, you are *also* harmed by their warped behaviors and perceptions. When a person is sick, everyone suffers. A crisis can take place anywhere, with anyone, and when it happens, everyone wants empathy and no one wants to be the first to offer it. Offering yourself respite often means leaving someone else to fend for themselves, and what are the *social* consequences of that, then? You will always make someone mad. How does one choses the lesser of two evils, the right person to disappoint?

Specially in places where the perception is that those are individual issues, when, really, a good chunk of it is social. Specially in a moment where depression and anxiety are amongst the top concerns of the World Health Organization. 

Think about how this manifests in some of the characters in the show: Trinity is a bully; Robby makes the workplace a living hell; Baran feels the need to rely on new and, arguably, unethical tech to just *get through the day*.

Are we really impressed that we're all going a bit nuts? Everyone is too exhausted by their own disaster to be _able_ to care. And isn't that on the wrong side of fascinating, how a fandom can become, quite easily, a microcosmos of the things it's object is trying to discuss?

### Some caveats and inconclusions

*The Pitt* is a show that is in a particularly disastrous position of being really popular for a piece of media that deals with human complexities that no one truly knows what to do with. It commits the crime of having painfully human characters in an era where people have been pretty much brainwashed into having fights over nothing by the vehicles they use to express their feelings and opinions.

Does the show commit other crimes? Possibly. Probably. Insert something about ethical consumption under capitalism, here. Something else about the impacts of the choices made by powerful people, whatever the hell that means, nowadays.

Truth of the matter: as of right now, I don't care. A conversation about what it means to have power, and what power even is, is entirely out of scope—not that it matters. It all gets lost in translation, ever quickening news cycles, hashtags and *volume*. Collectively, we've forgotten how to make these conversations take any effect. The means by which we used to *make* them effective has changed. We lost our grip on *meaning*. Not our grip on *importance*, but what to do with the important things. Our grip on context, scale. It's all messy, now, isn't it?

As such, my ramblings have their limits, as ramblings often do. My scope and space of experience is very limited, and, frankly—I'm not staying longer than necessary in the looney bin. What you quickly learn, in environments like this, is to act like one does in a corporate job: you block all the things that are not of immediate interest. You arrive. You take what you need. Do what you can. And then you *turn tail and you leave*.

So I left.

But I've been also sent other things. An Instagram reel of a doctor and her non-doctor husband, reacting to the show. She makes her comments—menial complaints about menial inaccuracies, impressively fast diagnostics, general humor. It's all very simple, less to do with the real core of show, more with the medicine. Until, at some point, her husband comments on something regarding the characters, some mistake, or some less than perfectly kind attitude. And this woman, god bless her, looks at him and very indignantly says *"This is a rough day!"*

Sometimes that's all you need to hear, isn't it? It's all in this small acknowledgment. This is a rough fucking day. I'm not sure why it had such an impact on me, hearing this woman who is a doctor, who knows the pressure and hardship that's been shown to us on screen so personally, so realistically, just say it that simply.

It's a rough fucking day.

Something about it reminded me that not all days are a disaster. That there *are* still safe places, and that we can all find them. That empathy, while limited, is one of those things where a little goes a long way. That there's places and people who will understand you, who are in the right place and moment for it. And people who won't. You find the first. You forgive the later. You rest. And yes, you piss people off doing it, if you have to.

It's a rough day. And still, we find ourselves trying. Failing—hard. But trying. That has to amount for something, right?

No, scratch that. I know, for a fact, that it does.
